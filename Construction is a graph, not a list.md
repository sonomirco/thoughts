Construction is a web of dependencies.

Each project consists of interconnected elements, in which one design choice relies on a code constraint that, in turn, depends on product lead times influenced by a procurement decision made months earlier by someone who never communicated with the engineer. Change one part of this web, and the effects ripple through the entire project. This isn't just a matter of construction complexity; it's the essence of construction.

Yet we manage these intricate relationships with the simplest tools available—bar charts, spreadsheets, and Kanban columns arranged from left to right, as if the work were a linear queue rather than a network. We take a three-dimensional system of relationships and flatten it onto a two-dimensional surface, then act surprised when unexpected issues arise.

Daniel Miessler captured this concept accurately: every company operates as a graph of algorithms a series of steps that break down into smaller steps, all interconnected. While construction has always functioned this way, with critical-path networks illustrating dependencies for decades, the difference now lies in our ability to keep the entire graph in view and continually analyse it, rather than letting the connections become outdated as projects evolve and relying solely on a project manager's memory.

Instead of logging in to check the schedule, you inform a system of what you truly care about whether you want to accelerate a payment milestone or are concerned about exposure to a claim and it monitors project data continuously, providing a comprehensive analysis when it detects anything noteworthy. It essentially acts like a Monday-morning briefing before the meeting, always vigilant.

Imagine applying the same idea to model health, creating a monitor for both the design and commercial aspects.

Now, consider the implications of a single change. If a specification error arises in an MEP submittal, rather than just noting one line on a clash report, you can visualise the entire ripple effect: how it alters the spatial clearance in the model, impacts procurement lead times for air handlers, shifts the project timeline, and ultimately affects the owner's thirty-year operating cost. One error, traced throughout the life of the building.

This is where I believe the construction industry undervalues its potential. The real benefit isn't merely a faster report or a more efficient team. **Once we can visualise the entire graph, coordination transitions from being an afterthought to becoming the core of the work itself.** The dependencies are tangible, and mistakes can be costly, so the true transformation involves teams engaging in earlier cross-disciplinary conversations that previously discovered issues too late. This change in collaboration will eventually influence how contracts are structured more than it will alter any single task.
