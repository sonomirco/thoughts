We keep trying to install AI agents.

When someone joins a team, nobody hands them the keys on day one. Someone provisions their access to the right drives, project servers, and models. A colleague sits down with them to explain the unwritten rules: for instance, that a specific client always reworks the brief at stage three, that the local authority enforces stricter regulations than the national code, or which subcontractor should be monitored closely for scheduling. Their early work is scrutinised closely. They gain autonomy through support, not by being activated.

An AI agent requires all of these steps. Unfortunately, many firms overlook them. They purchase the license, point the AI at the work, and expect finished output by lunchtime.

As a result, the agent proceeds without the necessary context, only faster and with more confidence. It addresses symptoms, not causes. If you ask it to fix a crash, it may say "done," while quietly creating four new problems because it wasn't informed that the entire approach was flawed. It will continue to loop in that cycle, unaware it's lost. Someone needs to evaluate the output and determine whether to dismantle it and start over.

This oversight role is evolving. It's not back-office IT; it's a construction-literate individual who collaborates closely with the project team and manages the AI much like a good lead manages a new team member. This includes provisioning its access, questioning its logic, auditing its output before it reaches a deliverable, and teaching it the specific codes and dynamics of that particular project.

**Automation doesn't eliminate management; it simply shifts the focus from managing people to managing models.**

A reliable system requires two components working together: one that strictly follows the rules and another that understands what works on the ground. The first can be purchased, but the second only comes from supervision; there's no shortcut for that, especially in construction.

Which quietly changes what our own value is. Less of the rote work — the agent takes that. More of the thing a good engineer already does without calling it a skill: bringing someone new up to speed, noticing where they've gone wrong, and knowing when something needs rebuilding from first principles.

We spent years learning to use these tools. The next step is learning how to supervise them — setting up the guardrails and the context that let them operate the right way.
