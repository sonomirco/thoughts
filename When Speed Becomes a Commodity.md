For most of its history, AEC has had one definition of efficiency. Faster delivery, lower cost. That definition is so embedded in how the industry talks about itself that it's easy to forget it's a choice.

Mithu Storoni made the point on a recent HBR conversation: our idea of efficiency comes from the assembly line, where productivity meant how much quantity you could produce per unit time. That's the model AEC inherited and never really updated.

The problem is that the quantity side of that equation is being absorbed. AI is making "faster and cheaper" available to everyone, which means it stops being a competitive position and starts being the floor. Once it happens to everyone, what's actually left?

You can already see the answer forming, awkwardly, inside drawing packages. We generate more drawings now because it's easy to. Tools let us spin up views in a click, so we add one for every angle, every condition, every "just in case." The package gets bigger. The information starts repeating itself. The same dimension shows up in three views, not all of them quite agreeing with each other. The contractor opens the package and has to do the editorial work that we didn't.

This isn't a tooling problem. It's that the discipline of producing less has quietly disappeared, because the cost of producing more dropped close to zero. Nobody really asks "what is the minimal set of views that lets this thing get built well?" anymore. We just add.

**When speed becomes a commodity, value stops coming from how much you produce. It starts coming from what you choose not to produce.**

That's the harder conversation AEC is going to have to have with itself. Storoni's reframe lands directly here: humans now influence productivity through the quality of their output, not the quantity. In our industry, that means fewer and sharper artefacts that actually do the work — clearer drawing packages, models well defined with coherent metadata that answer the right questions, decisions that don't get buried under the weight of their own artefacts.

Efficiency was always meant to be a means. Somewhere along the way it became the goal. Once everyone is fast, the question stops being "how quickly can we deliver" and becomes "what is actually worth delivering." That's a much harder question, and it's the one the industry hasn't really started asking.

Neil spent twenty years following one problem through every position in the delivery chain. His diagnosis of why AEC keeps adding tools instead of cutting them is the structural explanation for the discipline problem above.
