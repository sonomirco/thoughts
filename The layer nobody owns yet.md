One of the most common concerns about navigating AI is the difficulty in finding a vendor-neutral partner for assistance.

This concern is understandable. The individuals AI companies embed within your organisation for deep integration with their product are inherently biased. Their primary role is to ensure a seamless integration with a single platform.

Currently, this presents a problem. No one can confidently predict which AI service will be the best fit in two years, as the landscape is still evolving. Having options means the ability to choose the vendor that suits your needs best once the dust settles is genuinely valuable, and tight integration can quietly undermine that flexibility.

I'm increasingly seeing a new type of company emerging to fill this gap. A new generation of startups is helping firms establish AI workflows and educate teams about the technology's capabilities and limitations, all without being tied to any specific platform. Rather than selling you a vendor, they are building the integration layer that operates above all of them.

Internal teams can also play this role; they may already have the right personnel in place. AEC already possess people with the skills necessary to break down tasks, allocate resources, and manage dependencies. These skills are directly applicable to orchestrating AI agents. Nikki Barua at Flipwork explains it this way: the discipline remains unchanged; only the resource does — you're now allocating work to agents instead of humans.

**The most sustainable position in the transition to AI doesn't belong to the vendor or the client; it belongs to whoever controls the integration layer in between.**

The strategic question to start to discuss is: "Where does our orchestration layer reside, and who controls it?"
