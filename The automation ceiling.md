Construction has always been a place of creative builders.

What has changed is the time it now takes to draft little prototypes or automations. These tend to emerge under pressure, once delivery increases in complexity — they're not planned, they're an instinctive reaction to a problem that needs fixing, and now we can produce those solutions and deploy them on projects. That is genuinely useful, and it is happening across the industry.

What is also happening, and not only in construction, is that most of it lands on individual steps. AI gets layered onto an existing process without touching the workflow it sits inside. The step gets faster. The sequence stays the same.

Looking at a close market, real estate, what McKinsey documented is that organisations are deploying tools to read leases, triage maintenance requests, and support investment decisions, but in many cases the efforts remain fragmented — focused on improving individual steps rather than transforming how work actually gets done. The organisations generating the largest returns are the ones that redesigned end-to-end workflows: taking a process like financial reporting all the way from data aggregation to bespoke stakeholder output, cutting 60 to 80 per cent of the time the entire process takes. That kind of result is not available from automating a single task inside an unreformed sequence.

**The framing that comes out of that work is useful: enterprise is too large to transform at once, and use cases are too small to connect. The unit that creates real value is the full domain. Redesigning that end-to-end, with agents and humans working across the whole sequence, is a different exercise from adding a faster step at one end of it.**

There is also a direction of travel to account for. As building gets faster and cheaper, the justification for generic solutions weakens. If a workflow designed for how your organisation actually operates can be built in days rather than months, off-the-shelf generic stops being the sensible default. It becomes the familiar one. The expectation of what a tool should know about your specific context, your specific project, your specific constraints, is moving. Teams that are still thinking in point solutions will feel that shift before they understand it.

What we are going to hit is not a ceiling of solutions but a ceiling of incremental improvement inside an unreformed process. The wave of small automations is just starting. What is necessary is to map where the real friction lives — and ask what it would mean to redesign the sequence rather than just speed up the steps. That question will define a different set of options on the other side of it.
